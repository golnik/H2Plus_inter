Introduction

Advances in laser technology over the past several decades have revolutionized the study of properties and behavior of matter by enabling direct observation of quantum dynamics in atomic, molecular, and solid state systems with unprecedented temporal and spatial resolution. At the quantum level, this dynamics arises from the formation of coherent superpositions of quantum states and their subsequent evolution governed by interactions among electrons, nuclei, and external electromagnetic fields. The purpose of this interactive demonstration is to introduce the fundamental concepts of quantum dynamics through the example of the hydrogen molecular cation, one of the simplest systems that exhibits coupled electronic and nuclear motion.

We begin by introducing mathematical formalism required to describe a molecular system at the quantum level, including the construction of the Hamiltonian, the nature of electronic and coupled electron-nuclear dynamics, and the theoretical framework used to describe and solve the quantum dynamics of molecules. These concepts are then illustrated using the hydrogen molecular cation through the determination of its stationary states, the emergence of purely electronic dynamics from coherent superposition of electronic states, the formation and propagation of nuclear wave packets, the role of electronic coherences, and, ultimately, the full quantum dynamics arising from the coupled evolution of electronic and nuclear degrees of freedom.

Basic Concepts of Electronic and Electron-Nuclear Dynamics

To describe the properties and behavior of a molecule, we solve the fundamental quantum mechanical equation of motion, known as the time-dependent Schrödinger equation: \begin{equation} \label{eq:TDSE} i \hbar \frac{\partial \Psi(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R},t)}{\partial t} = \hat{H}(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R}) \Psi(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R},t), \end{equation} where \(\hbar\) is the reduced Planck constant, \(\mathbf{r}\) and \(\mathbf{R}\) represent the electronic and nuclear coordinates, respectively, and \(\Psi(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R},t)\) is the time-dependent molecular wavefunction describing the quantum state of the system determined by the Hamiltonian operator \(\hat{H}(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R})\). For a field-free molecular system, the Hamiltonian operator can be written in the following form \begin{equation} \label{eq:H} \hat{H}(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R}) = \hat{T}_n + \underbrace{\hat{T}_e + V(\mathbf{r})}_{\hat{H}_e(\mathbf{r};\mathbf{R})}, \end{equation} where \(\hat{T}_n\) and \(\hat{T}_e\) are the nuclear and electronic kinetic energy terms, respectively, and \(V(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R})\) is the potential energy describing the Coulomb interactions among the electrons and nuclei.

In Eq. (\(\ref{eq:H}\)), we introduced the electronic Hamiltonian operator \(\hat{H}_e(\mathbf{r};\mathbf{R})\), which determines the behavior of electrons for a fixed nuclear configuration. In this formulation, the nuclear coordinates \(\mathbf{R}\) enter the electronic Hamiltonian only as parameters, enabling the definition of a separate Schrödinger equation for the electronic subsystem in which the nuclei are treated as stationary: \begin{equation} \label{eq:TDSE_e} i \hbar \frac{\partial \Phi_{\mathbf{R}}(\mathbf{r},t)}{\partial t} = \hat{H}_e(\mathbf{r};\mathbf{R}) \Phi_{\mathbf{R}}(\mathbf{r},t), \end{equation} where \(\Phi_\mathbf{R}(\mathbf{r},t)\) is the time-dependent electronic wavefunction for a given nuclear configuration. To simplify the following discussion, the dependence on the nuclear coordinates \(\mathbf{R}\) will be omitted from the notation where it does not lead to ambiguity. Thus, the electronic wavefunctions and the related quantities will be written in a compact form while retaining their parametric dependence on the nuclear degrees of freedom.

A convenient way to solve Eq. (\(\ref{eq:TDSE_e}\)) is to start with the time-independent Schrödinger equation for the electronic Hamiltonian: \begin{equation} \label{eq:SE_He} \hat{H}_e(\mathbf{r}) \Phi_I(\mathbf{r}) = E_I \Phi_I(\mathbf{r}). \end{equation} The obtained set of stationary electronic states, \(\{\Phi_I(\mathbf{r})\}\), and their corresponding energies, \(E_I\), forms a complete basis for representing the time-dependent electronic wavefunction: \begin{equation} \label{eq:Phi} \Phi(\mathbf{r},t) = \sum_I c_I(t) \Phi_I(\mathbf{r}), \end{equation} where \(c_I(t)\) are the time-dependent expansion coefficients. Substituting this expansion into the time-dependent electronic Schrödinger equation and exploiting the orthonormality of the stationary states yields \begin{equation} \label{eq:Phi_sol} \Phi(\mathbf{r},t) = \sum_I c_I \exp\left({-\frac{i}{\hbar} E_I t}\right) \Phi_I(\mathbf{r}), \end{equation} where the time-independent coefficients \(c_I\) determine the weights of the electronic eigenstates in the created superposition.

The time-dependent electronic wavefunction, \(\Phi(\mathbf{r},t)\), provides complete information about the electron motion in the system and enables the evaluation of a wide range of electronic properties. In particular, the time-dependent electron density offers an intuitive visualization of how the electronic charge redistributes throughout the molecule over time. It can be obtained by evaluating the expectation value of the electron density operator, \(\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})\): \begin{equation} \label{eq:rho} \begin{split} \rho(\mathbf{r},t) & = \int \Phi^{*}(\mathbf{r},t) \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}) \Phi(\mathbf{r},t) d\mathbf{r} \\ & = \sum_I |c_I|^2 \rho_{II}(\mathbf{r}) + \sum_{I,J} c^{*}_I c_J \exp \left[{-\frac{i}{\hbar} \left( E_J-E_I \right) t}\right] \rho_{IJ}(\mathbf{r}), \end{split} \end{equation} where \(\rho_{IJ}(\mathbf{r})=\int \Phi^{*}_I(\mathbf{r}) \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}) \Phi_I(\mathbf{r}) d\mathbf{r}\) are the matrix elements of the electron density operator in the basis of stationary electronic states. The diagonal terms \((I=J)\) correspond to the stationary electron densities associated with individual electronic states, whereas the off-diagonal terms \((I\neq J)\) represent the transition electron densities.

The basis of stationary electronic states, \(\{\Phi_I(\mathbf{r})\}\), also provides a convenient representation for solving the full time-dependent Schrödinger equation. Expanding the total molecular wavefunction as \begin{equation} \label{eq:fullWF_expansion} \Psi(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R},t) = \sum_I c_I \chi_I(\mathbf{R},t) \Phi_I(\mathbf{r}), \end{equation} with \(\chi_I(\mathbf{R},t)\) denoting the time-dependent nuclear wave packets associated with the corresponding electronic states, allows the full time-dependent Schrödinger equation, Eq. (\(\ref{eq:TDSE}\)), to be transformed into a set of coupled equations of motion for the nuclear wave packets.

Recalling that the electronic states and their corresponding energies retain an implicit parametric dependence on the nuclear coordinates, such that \(\Phi_I(\mathbf{r}) \equiv \Phi_I(\mathbf{r};\mathbf{R})\) and \(E_I \equiv E_I(\mathbf{R})\), we now consider the limit in which the dependence of the electronic states on the nuclear geometry is sufficiently weak to approximate the electronic wavefunctions as fixed, while retaining the full dependence of the electronic energies on the nuclear coordinates. Under this approximation, the nonadiabatic couplings between different electronic states can be neglected, and the equations of motion for the nuclear wave packets reduce to \begin{equation} i \hbar \frac{\partial \chi_I(\mathbf{R},t)}{\partial t} = \left[ \hat{T}_n + E_I(\mathbf{R}) \right] \chi_I(\mathbf{R},t). \end{equation} In this context, each nuclear wave packet evolves independently within a single electronic state without transferring population to other states. The electronic energies \(E_I(\mathbf{R})\), viewed as functions of the nuclear coordinates, define the potential energy surfaces (PESs), which determine the forces acting on the nuclei and thereby govern their motion. Consequently, the coupled electron-nuclear problem is reduced to solving the electronic Schrödinger equation to obtain the potential energy surfaces, followed by the propagation of nuclear wave packets on these surfaces. This separation of electronic and nuclear motion forms the basis of the Born–Oppenheimer approximation and underlies much of modern theoretical chemistry and molecular quantum dynamics.

Similarly to the case of purely electronic dynamics, the total molecular wavefunction \(\Psi(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R},t)\) can be used to calculate time-dependent molecular properties, including the dynamics of the electron density. Evaluating the expectation value of the electron density operator yields: \begin{equation} \label{eq:rho_full} \begin{split} \rho(\mathbf{r},t) & = \int \int \Psi^{*}(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R},t) \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}) \Psi(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R},t) d\mathbf{r} d\mathbf{R} \\ & = \sum_I |c_I|^2 \rho_{II}(\mathbf{r}) + \sum_{I,J} c^{*}_I c_J \chi_{IJ}(t) \rho_{IJ}(\mathbf{r}), \end{split} \end{equation} where the quantity \(\chi_{IJ}(t) = \int \chi^{*}_I(\mathbf{R},t) \chi_J(\mathbf{R},t) d\mathbf{R}\) represents the overlap between nuclear wave packets associated with different electronic states and is referred to as the electronic coherence.

In contrast to the purely electronic case, expression Eq. (\(\ref{eq:rho_full}\)) includes an additional integration over the nuclear coordinates and therefore accounts for the influence of nuclear motion on the electronic density. Substituting the expansion of the molecular wavefunction in terms of electronic states and nuclear wave packets yields contributions from both the populations of individual electronic states and the electronic coherences between them, while the time evolution of the nuclear wave packets introduces the effects of nuclear motion. Thus, the time-dependent electron density provides a direct representation of the coupled electron-nuclear dynamics.

Hydrogen Cation

In the case of the hydrogen molecular cation, which consists of a single electron and two protons, the molecular Hamiltonian takes the particularly simple form \begin{equation} \label{eq:H_hydrogen} \hat{H}(r_1,r_2,R) = \overbrace{-\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu_n}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial R^2}}^{\hat{T}_n} \quad \underbrace{ \overbrace{-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}\nabla_e^2}^{\hat{T}_e} \quad \overbrace{-\frac{e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \left( \frac{1}{r_1} +\frac{1}{r_2} \right) +\frac{e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0R} }^{V(r_1,r_2,R)} }_{\hat{H}_e(r_1,r_2;R)}, \end{equation} where \(R\) is the internuclear distance, \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) are the distances between the electron and nuclei \(1\) and \(2\), respectively, \(\mu_n\) is the reduced mass of the two protons, and \(m_e\) is the electron mass. This form of the Hamiltonian results from expressing the three-body problem in terms of the internal coordinates after separating the center-of-mass motion. The electronic Laplacian \(\nabla_e^2\) in these internal coordinates takes a rather complicated form due to the non-orthogonality of the chosen coordinate system and the resulting coupling between the electronic and nuclear degrees of freedom. The explicit form of the Laplacian and its derivation are omitted here for simplicity and can be explored in more detail in Refs.

To solve the time-independent electronic Schrödinger equation, Eq. \((\ref{eq:SE_He})\), we employ the linear combination of atomic orbitals (LCAO) method, in which the molecular electronic wavefunctions are expanded at each nuclear geometry in a basis of atomic orbitals: \begin{equation} \Phi_I(\mathbf{r}) = \sum_\mu c_{\mu}^{I}\phi_\mu(\mathbf{r}), \end{equation} where \(\phi_\mu(\mathbf{r})\) are the atomic basis functions and \(c_{\mu}^{I}\) are the corresponding expansion coefficients. In this interactive demonstration, we adopt the simplest possible basis consisting of two hydrogen \(1s\) atomic orbitals \(\phi_1(\mathbf{r})\) and \(\phi_2(\mathbf{r})\), each described by a simple exponential function \begin{equation} \phi_{1s}(\mathbf{r})= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}} \exp\left(-|\mathbf{r}|\right). \end{equation} The basis functions are obtained by placing these orbitals on the corresponding nuclei separated by the internuclear distance \(R\). Such a formulation provides a minimal basis for describing the electronic states of the hydrogen molecular cation.

Representing the electronic Hamiltonian, \(\hat{H}_e(r_1,r_2;R)\), in the basis of atomic orbitals and applying the variational principle yields two molecular electronic states. The electronic energies of these states are \begin{equation} E_B(R)=\frac{H_{11}(R)+H_{12}(R)} {1+S(R)}, \qquad E_A(R)=\frac{H_{11}(R)-H_{12}(R)} {1-S(R)}, \end{equation} and the corresponding normalized wavefunctions are \begin{equation} \Phi_B(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\phi_1(\mathbf{r})+\phi_2(\mathbf{r})} {\sqrt{2\left(1+S(R)\right)}}, \qquad \Phi_A(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\phi_1(\mathbf{r})-\phi_2(\mathbf{r})} {\sqrt{2\left(1-S(R)\right)}}. \end{equation} Here, \(H_{ij}(R)=\langle\phi_i|\hat{H}_e|\phi_j\rangle \) are the matrix elements of the electronic Hamiltonian and \(S(R)=\langle\phi_1|\phi_2\rangle\) is the overlap integral between the two atomic orbitals. The lower-energy PES \(E_B(R)\) and the associated wavefunction \(\Phi_B(\mathbf{r})\) correspond to the bonding (\(B\)) electronic state, while the higher-energy pair of \(E_A(R)\) and \(\Phi_A(\mathbf{r})\) represent the antibonding (\(A\)) electronic state.
