This is an interactive tool that explores the ultrafast quantum bonding dynamics in the H2+ molecule. This is a system with 2 protons and 1 electron.

To use this tool you can navigate to different screens at various dynamics from static to full dynamics. For the Static and Electron Dynamics sections you will be able to change the radius by either moving the slider or changing the number in the text box. Doing this will change the radius between the two protons/nuclei in the system. For all dynamics screens you will see a time and superposition block. The two 'c' constants change the superposition state of the molecule. In this system we are assuming that we ionized an H2 molecule to some superposition state between the bonding and antibonding states of the H2+ system. This state of superposition can change having more or less in the bonding state versus the antibonding state. The time slider allows you to explore how the bonding dynamics actually works though time at an ultrafast scale of femtoseconds(10-15 seconds).

For this interactive we use the Schrödinger equation: \(i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = \hat{H} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)\) :to solve for statics and dynamics in the H2+ molecule purely quantum mechanically.

For statics we use the time independent Schrödinger equation: \(\hat{H} \psi(\mathbf{r}) = E \psi(\mathbf{r})\) : Then using the Born-Oppenheimer approximation and making all movement on the one dimensional z-axis we get the following hamiltonian: \(H=-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e} \nabla^2-\frac{e^2}{4 \pi \epsilon_0 r_A}-\frac{e^2}{4 \pi \epsilon_0 r_B}+\frac{e^2}{4 \pi \epsilon_0 R}\). Using this hamiltonian and the LCAO approximation technique as seen below we can get the following equation for the potential energy surfaces where R is the radius between the two protons/nuclei and r1 and r2 are the distance from the electron to proton 1 and two respectively: \[E(\mathbf{R})=\langle \phi (r_1)\vert \hat H\vert\phi (r_1)\rangle\pm\langle\phi (r_1)\vert\hat H\vert\phi (r_2)\rangle\] This then when solved gives us the following: \[E_1=\frac{q_{el}}{4\pi\epsilon_0R}*\frac{(1+\frac{R}{a_0})e^{-2\frac{R}{a_0}}+(1-\frac{2}{3}(\frac{R}{a_0})^2e^{-\frac{R}{a_0}})}{1+(1+\frac{R}{a_0}+\frac{1}{3}(\frac{R}{a_0})^2)e^{-\frac{R}{a_0}}}\] \[E_2=\frac{q_{el}}{4\pi\epsilon_0R}*\frac{(1+\frac{R}{a_0})e^{-2\frac{R}{a_0}}-(1-\frac{2}{3}(\frac{R}{a_0})^2e^{-\frac{R}{a_0}})}{1-(1+\frac{R}{a_0}+\frac{1}{3}(\frac{R}{a_0})^2)e^{-\frac{R}{a_0}}}\] :where E1 and E2 are the potential energy surfaces for the bonding and antibonding states respectively. qel is the charge of an electron, ϵ0 is the vacuum permittivity constant, and a0 is bohr radius.

We use one more approximation technique called Linear Combination of Atomic Orbitals (LCAO) which says: \[\psi=\sum_{r} c_r \phi_r\]\[\psi_\pm \approx \phi_a \pm \phi_b\] :where \(\phi_a\) is the wavefunction about the nucleus on the first hydrogen, and \(\phi_b\) is the wavefunction about the nucleus of the second hydrogen in the bond. We use the wave function: \[\phi(r)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}}e^{-\mathbf{r}}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}}e^{-\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}}\]: where we assume that z=0 is the middle in between the two protons giving us: \(z_a=z-\frac{R}{2}\) and \(z_b=z+\frac{R}{2}\) : Then we can now use: \(P(\mathbf{r})=\vert\psi(\mathbf{r})\vert^2\) :where 'P' is the electronic probability density. Now since we only want one moving axis (z), we must integrate out x and y. We use our LCAO approximation for the bonding and antibonding states (\(\psi_+\) and \(\psi_-\) respectively), with a normalization constant 'N' to make the final function integrate out to 1, allowing us to get the following for the bonding state: \[P_{bond}(z)=N^2\int_0^{\infty}\int_0^{\infty}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}}\left(e^{-\sqrt{x^2+y^2+\left(z+\frac{R}{2}\right)^2}}+e^{-\sqrt{x^2+y^2+\left(z-\frac{R}{2}\right)^2}}\right)\right)^2\,dxdy\] \[P_{bond}(z)=\frac{N^2}{\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\infty}r\left(e^{-\sqrt{r^2+\left(z+\frac{R}{2}\right)^2}}+e^{-\sqrt{r^2+\left(z-\frac{R}{2}\right)^2}}\right)^2\,drd\theta\] \[P_{bond}(z)=2N^2\left(\int_0^{\infty}re^{-2\sqrt{r^2+\left(z+\frac{R}{2}\right)^2}}\,dr+\int_0^{\infty}re^{-\sqrt{r^2+\left(z+\frac{R}{2}\right)^2}-\sqrt{r^2+\left(z-\frac{R}{2}\right)^2}}\,dr+\int_0^{\infty}re^{-2\sqrt{r^2+\left(z-\frac{R}{2}\right)^2}}\,dr\right)\] We are going to say \(j=\int_0^{\infty}re^{-\sqrt{r^2+\left(z+\frac{R}{2}\right)^2}-\sqrt{r^2+\left(z-\frac{R}{2}\right)^2}}\,dr\) since it must be solved numerically. Then using a u-sub for the first and last integrals in the step above of \(u=\sqrt{r^2+\left(z+\frac{R}{2}\right)^2}\) where \(u\,du=r\,dr\) and \(v=\sqrt{r^2+\left(z-\frac{R}{2}\right)^2}\) where \(v\,dv=r\,dr\) we get: \[P_{bond}(z)=2N^2\left(\int_0^{\infty}ue^{-2u}\,du+\int_0^{\infty}ve^{-2v}\,dv+j\right)\] \[P_{bond}(z)=N^2\left(\frac{1}{2}e^{-2\vert z+\frac{R}{2}\vert}\left(1+2\left\vert z+\frac{R}{2}\right\vert\right)+\frac{1}{2}e^{-2\vert z-\frac{R}{2}\vert}\left(1+2\left\vert z-\frac{R}{2}\right\vert\right)+j\right)\] When we do this for the antibonding state we get: \[P_{anti}(z)=N^2\left(\frac{1}{2}e^{-2\vert z+\frac{R}{2}\vert}\left(1+2\left\vert z+\frac{R}{2}\right\vert\right)+\frac{1}{2}e^{-2\vert z-\frac{R}{2}\vert}\left(1+2\left\vert z-\frac{R}{2}\right\vert\right)-j\right)\] We can solve for N by integrating from \(-\infty\) to \(\infty\) with respect to z and setting it equal to 1 to solve for N. Doing this we get: \[N^2_{bond}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\left(1+e^{-R}\left(1+R+\frac{R^2}{3}\right)\right)}}\quad\text{and}\quad N^2_{anti}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\left(1-e^{-R}\left(1+R+\frac{R^2}{3}\right)\right)}}\] These two equations give the electronic probability densisty graphs seen in the static section where R is the radius between the nuclei.

To find the electronic probability density curve for the Electron Dynamics section we say: \[\Psi = c_1\vert \psi_+ \rangle + c_2\vert \psi_- \rangle\] Then we use the following equation with time dependence to find the curve: \[\langle \Psi \vert p \vert \Psi \rangle\] \[=c_1^2\langle \psi_+\vert e^{-iE_1t}pe^{-iE_1t}\vert \psi_+\rangle + c_1c_2\langle \psi_+\vert e^{-iE_1t}pe^{-iE_2t}\vert \psi_-\rangle + c_2c_1\langle \psi_-\vert e^{-iE_2t}pe^{-iE_1t}\vert \psi_+\rangle + c_2^2\langle \psi_-\vert e^{-iE_2t}pe^{-iE_2t}\vert \psi_-\rangle\] \[\text{Because we are working in only the real plane the middle two terms are the same:}\] \[=c_1^2\langle \psi_+\vert e^{-iE_1t}pe^{-iE_1t}\vert \psi_+\rangle + 2c_1c_2\langle \psi_+\vert e^{-iE_1t}pe^{-iE_2t}\vert \psi_-\rangle + c_2^2\langle \psi_-\vert e^{-iE_2t}pe^{-iE_2t}\vert \psi_-\rangle\] \[=\vert c_1\vert^2 \vert \psi_+\vert^2 + 2c_1c_2\psi_+\psi_-\cos(t(E_1-E_2)) + \vert c_2\vert^2 \vert \psi_-\vert^2\] \(c_1 \,\text{and}\, c_2\) represent the population in each of the bonding and antibonding states respectively where \(1=c_1^2+c_2^2\). \(E_1\) represents the energy at some radius on the potential energy curve for the bonding state and \(E_2\) for the antibonding state. Notice that we already solved for \(\vert \psi_+\vert^2\ \,\text{and}\, \vert \psi_-\vert^2\) as \(P_{bond}(z) \,\text{and}\, P_{anti}(z)\). This gives us the electronic probability density function for Electron Dynamics, showing how the probability function changes over time in some state of superposition.

To find the nucleat probability density curves for the Nuclear Dynamics section we used the Multi-Configurable Time Dependent Hartree algorrithm (MCTDH) to simulate and pre-generate the nuclear wavepacket data because it would have been too complicated to do it in the browser. We used the command showd1d84 to generate the wavepackets, and the command expect84 to generate the expected radius and momentum.

To get the electronic probability densisty chart for the Full Dynamics section we say: \[\Psi = c_1\vert\chi_+,\psi_+ \rangle + c_2\vert\chi_-,\psi_- \rangle\] Then we again use the following equation (the 'p' terms are the same as above with the time dependent part, but to save space it has been collapsed down to just p): \[\langle \Psi \vert p \vert \Psi \rangle\] \[=\vert c_1\vert^2\langle\psi_+\vert p\vert\psi_+\rangle\langle\chi_+\vert\chi_+\rangle + c_1c_2\langle\psi_+\vert p \vert\psi_-\rangle\langle\chi_+\vert\chi_-\rangle + c_2c_1\langle\psi_-\vert p\vert\psi_+\rangle\langle\chi_-\vert\chi_+\rangle + \vert c_2\vert^2\langle\psi_-\vert p\vert\psi_-\rangle\langle\chi_-\vert\chi_-\rangle\] \[\text{Because we are working in only the real plane the middle two terms are the same:}\] \[=c_1^2\langle\psi_+\vert p\vert\psi_+\rangle\langle\chi_+\vert\chi_+\rangle + 2c_1c_2\langle\psi_+\vert p \vert\psi_-\rangle\langle\chi_+\vert\chi_-\rangle + c_2^2\langle\psi_-\vert p\vert\psi_-\rangle\langle\chi_-\vert\chi_-\rangle\] \[=c_1^2\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\vert\chi_+\vert^2\vert\psi_+\vert^2\,dR + 2c_1c_2\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\chi_+\chi_-\psi_+\psi_-\cos(t(E_1-E_2))\,dR\ + c_2^2\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\vert\chi_-\vert^2\vert\psi_-\vert^2\,dR\] Because these integrals are not analytically solvable, we use a summation to get an approximation where \(r^* = (0.1+0.0486328125*k)\text{, and}\,\Delta R = 0.0486328125\) and t is time: \[\sum_{k=0}^{512}[c_1^2\vert\chi_+(r^*,t)\vert^2\vert\psi_+(r^*)\vert^2 + 2c_1c_2\chi_+(r^*,t)\chi_-(r^*,t)\psi_+(r^*)\psi_-(r^*)\cos(t[E_1(r^*)-E_2(r^*)]) + c_2^2\vert\chi_-(r^*,t)\vert^2\vert\psi_-(r^*)\vert^2]*\Delta R\]
