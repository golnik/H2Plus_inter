Summary: In molecular systems, the total energy of the system is highly dependent on the distance between the nuclei which make up the molecule. As these nuclei move, the electronic clouds change shape, and the total energy of the system changes. Molecules can have different electronic "states" which correspond to different solutions of the Schrödinger equation. Here we show the energy of two states of H2+, the diatomic hydrogen cation. We also plot the electronic density for each state which tells the probability of finding an electron in space at a given position.

In quantum mechanics a system can be in a "superposition" of states. This means that there is some probability of finding the system in one of multiple states (the system is 'in multiple states at once'). When a molecule is prepared in a superposition of electronic states, the total electronic density is not just the sum of the two state's densities. This is because an interference yields a time dependent density. Here we show this density as a function of time, internuclear distance, and the superposition population of each electronic state.
In addition to electronic dynamics, molecules have nuclear dynamics defined by the gradient of the potential energy curve of the electronic state. Here we simulate the dynamics of the nuclear wavefunction using the Multi-Configurable Time Dependent Hartree algorrithm (MCTDH), assuming vertical ionization of the neutral ground state nuclear wavefunction. Because the bonding and antibonding electronic state's potential energy surfaces have a different shape, the nuclear wavefunction evolves differently for each, which we then show through tht expectation value for position and momentum.
To be able to do full quantum molecular dynamics we must combine electron and nuclear dynamics where they are "coupled".

This is an interactive tool that explores the ultrafast quantum bonding dynamics in the H2+ molecule. This is a system with 2 protons and 1 electron.

To use this tool you can navigate to different screens at various dynamics from static to full dynamics.

For the Static and Electron Dynamics sections you will be able to change the radius by either moving the slider or changing the number in the text box. Doing this will change the radius between the two protons in the system.

For all the screens except Static you will see a time and superposition block. The two 'c' constants change the superposition state of the molecule. In this system we are assuming that we ionized an H2 to some superposition state between the bonding and antibonding states of the H2+ system. This state of superposition can change having more or less in the bonding state versus the antibonding state. The time slider allows you to explore how the bonding dynamics actually works though time at an ultrafast scale of femtoseconds(10-15 seconds).

The Statics section just shows a basic probability density curve for the electron at two states and how they change when you change the radius. The Electron Dynamics section shows how the probability curve changes through time when the system is in a state of superposition between two states. The Nuclear Dynamics section shows how the protons in the H2+ system move through time when ionized from the ground state of an H2 system. Finally the Full Quantum Dynamics section combines the Electron Dynamics and the Nuclear Dynamics sections allowing you to see how both the protons and the electron density move and change through time.


The graphs may not always be zoomed or moved to what you want to see. You can change both the graphs zoom and viewport. Below are some of the things you can do to move around the graph as well as a demo graph to test on briefly
  • Zoom:
    • Click and drag to select the area to zoom in on or use the zoom in button at the top right corner of the graph.
    • Click and drag on the ends of either the x or y axes to zoom out and in.
    • Double click the graph to reset to deafult zoom or use the house button at the top right corner of the graph.
  • Pan: Click and drag on the numbered axes x and y to move the graph along the axis or use the pan button at the top right corner of the graph.
  • Graph Lines: If there is a legend you can hide and show specific graphs by clicking on the graph in the legend.

This plot shows the energy difference between the ground state and the first excited state of H2+, diatomic hydrogen cation. The energy is dependent on the internuclear distance(R)

The blue curve shows the energy of the molecule in a 'bonding' electronic state. This curve shows that at some radius R0 the energy is minimized, showing a stable equilibrium. This internuclear distance is the chemical bond distance of the molecule.

The orange curve shows the energy of the molecule in an 'antibonding' electronic state. The curve shows that there is no internuclear distance at which the energy is minimized; instead, the energy forever decreases as the internuclear distance increases, showing a dissociative state.

This plot shows the probability density of the electronic wavefunction corresponding to the bonding state. This density represents the liklihood that the elctron will be found in a particular region of space. The densty is concentrated in between the nuclei because the two atomic orbitals of hydrogen constructively interfere in this state

The red dots represent the nuclei/protons in the H2+ system.

This plot shows the probability density of the electronic wavefunction corresponding to the antibonding state. This density represents the liklihood that the elctron will be found in a particular region of space. The densty in between the nuclei is 0 because the two atomic orbitals of hydrogen destructively interfere in this state. The point at which the probability is 0 is called a bonding "node".

The red dots represent the nuclei/protons in the H2+ system.

This plot shows the probability that the electron will be in a particular space for a system in superposition between the bonding and antibonding states.

The red dots represent the nuclei/protons in the H2+ system.

The two constants c1 and c2 represent the superposition state when c1 is the bonding state and c2 is the antibonding state.

The graph shows the probability that the electron will be in that slice of space given respect to the nuclei/protons (red dots), the superposition in bonding and antibonding state, and time. We can see that as you change the superposition state to be either 100% bonding or 100% antibonding that it will represent each of the respective graphs from the Static screen regardless of time.

This plot shows the energy difference of the ground state and the energy to have the two protons some radius apart and the probability density of the nuclei.

The blue curve shows the energy needed for a bonding system to form. This shows that at some radius R0 there is a minimum energy which it would like to settle to. The Green curve with the blue shaded region represents the probability density of the radius between the nuclei with respect to time

The orange curve shows the energy needed for an antibonding system to form. This curve shows that there is no radius at which it will settle and instead that they will want to continue to drift forever. The Red curve with the orange shaded region represents the probability density of the radius between the nuclei with respect to time.

As you increase time, the density graphs will go from the initial position 1.4441 bohr to the forever increase the radius. For the antibonding state, this is easy to see as the graph will forever be decreasing, so the lowest energy point is infinity. For the bonding state however though there is an energy dip, because the energy in the system at 1.4441 bohr is greater than 0 it will go down into the dip and then forever try to reach an energy greater than 0 causing it to also disassociate

This shows the expected nuclear radius of the two graphs with respect to time. The blue graph (lower) represents the bonding system, and the orange graph (upper) represents the antibonding system. As time increases, both curves increase in radius due to the disassociation as explained in the probability density graph.

The green curve shows the average between the two states that the nuclei would be based on.

This shows the expected nuclear momentum of the two graphs with respect to time. The blue graph (lower) represents the bonding system, and the orange graph (upper) represents the antibonding system. As time increases, the orange curve spikes up then levels out due to the initial steep slope of the energy curve then leveling off like the energy curve.

The blue curve starts by increasing as it moves along the steep curve then after it reaches the minimum it will start to slow down to a point as the slope of the energy curve levels off as well

The green curve shows the average between the two states that the nuclei would be based on.
