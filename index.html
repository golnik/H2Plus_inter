\(H_2^{+}\) Interactive

Summary: In molecular systems, the total energy of the system is highly dependent on the distance between the nuclei which make up the molecule. As these nuclei move, the electronic clouds change shape, and the total energy of the system changes. Molecules can have different electronic "states" which correspond to different solutions of the Schrödinger equation. Here we show the energy of two states of H2+, the diatomic hydrogen cation. We also plot the electronic density for each state which tells the probability of finding an electron in space at a given position.

In quantum mechanics a system can be in a "superposition" of states. This means that there is some probability of finding the system in one of multiple states (the system is 'in multiple states at once'). When a molecule is prepared in a superposition of electronic states, the total electronic density is not just the sum of the two state's densities. This is because an interference yields a time dependent density. Here we show this density as a function of time, internuclear distance, and the superposition population of each electronic state.
In addition to electronic dynamics, molecules have nuclear dynamics defined by the gradient of the potential energy curve of the electronic state. Here we simulate the dynamics of the nuclear wavefunction using the Multi-Configurable Time Dependent Hartree algorithm (MCTDH), assuming vertical ionization of the neutral ground state nuclear wavefunction. Because the bonding and antibonding electronic state's potential energy surfaces have a different shape, the nuclear wavefunction evolves differently for each, which we then show through tht expectation value for position and momentum.
To be able to do full quantum molecular dynamics we must combine electron and nuclear dynamics where they are "coupled".

Welcome to the Quantum Dynamics Learning Tool!

Developers: Alex Klyber, Maximillian Thomas, and Nikolay Golubev

This interactive learning tool provides a visualization of ultrafast electron-nuclear dynamics in the \(H_2^{+}\) molecular ion. The interface is organized into multiple sections, each focusing on a specific aspect of the system and the underlying dynamical processes. Context-sensitive information icons accompanying the figures and interface elements provide detailed descriptions of the quantities being displayed and their physical interpretation. A comprehensive discussion of the theoretical framework, computational methodology, and the physical concepts underlying the presented results is available in the Theory section.

Adjust the system parameters and simulation time to explore the electronic and electron-nuclear dynamics under different conditions. You can return to this page at any time by selecting the About button.

This plot shows the energy difference between the ground state energy of a hydrogen atom and the first excited state of H2+, diatomic hydrogen cation. The energy is dependent on the internuclear distance(R)

The blue curve shows the energy of the molecule in a 'bonding' electronic state. This curve shows that at some radius R0 the energy is minimized, showing a stable equilibrium. This internuclear distance is the chemical bond distance of the molecule.

The orange curve shows the energy of the molecule in an 'antibonding' electronic state. The curve shows that there is no internuclear distance at which the energy is minimized; instead, the energy forever decreases as the internuclear distance increases, showing a dissociative state.

This plot shows the probability density of the electronic wavefunction corresponding to the bonding state. This density represents the likelihood that the electron will be found in a particular region of space. The densty is concentrated in between the nuclei because the two atomic orbitals of hydrogen constructively interfere in this state

The red dots represent the nuclei/protons in the H2+ system.

This plot shows the probability density of the electronic wavefunction corresponding to the antibonding state. This density represents the likelihood that the electron will be found in a particular region of space. The densty in between the nuclei is 0 because the two atomic orbitals of hydrogen destructively interfere in this state. The point at which the probability is 0 is called a bonding "node".

The red dots represent the nuclei/protons in the H2+ system.

This plot shows the probability density of the electronic wavefunction corresponding to the superposition of the bonding and antibonding states. This density represents the likelihood that the electron will be found in a particular region of space. The density oscillates across time with a period proportional to the energy difference between the two states.

The red dots represent the nuclei/protons in the H2+ system.

The two constants c1 and c2 represent the states of superposition where c1 represents the bonding state and c2 represents the antibonding state.

We can see that as the superposition states go to either 100% bonding or 100% antibonding that the probability density will become each of the respective graphs from the Static screen regardless of time.

This plot shows the energy difference between the ground state and the first excited state of H2+, diatomic hydrogen cation. The energy is dependent on the internuclear distance(R)

The blue curve shows the energy of the molecule in a 'bonding' electronic state. This curve shows that at some radius R0 the energy is minimized, showing a stable equilibrium. This internuclear distance is the chemical bond distance of the molecule. The Green curve with the blue shaded region represents the probability density of the internuclear distance with respect to time for the bonding state.

The orange curve shows the energy of the molecule in an 'antibonding' electronic state. The curve shows that there is no internuclear distance at which the energy is minimized; instead, the energy forever decreases as the internuclear distance increases, showing a dissociative state. The Red curve with the orange shaded region represents the probability density of the internuclear distance with respect to time for the bonding state.

As you increase time, the probability density graphs will go from the initial position, 1.4441 bohr, to infinity. For the antibonding state, this is easy to see as the graph will forever be decreasing, so the lowest energy point is infinity. For the bonding state however though there is an energy minimum, because the energy in the system at 1.4441 bohr is greater than 0 it will go through the dip and forever try to reach an energy greater than 0 causing it to also disassociate

This plot shows the expected internuclear radius of the two states with respect to time. The blue graph (lower) represents the bonding state, and the orange graph (upper) represents the antibonding state.

The green curve shows the average expected internuclear radius between the two states taking into account how much each state is populated.

This shows the expected nuclear momentum of the two states with respect to time. The blue graph (lower) represents the bonding state, and the orange graph (upper) represents the antibonding state.

As time increases, the antibonding curve spikes up then levels out due to the initial steep slope of the energy curve then levels off. The blue curve starts by increasing as it moves along the steep curve then after it reaches the energy minimum it will start to slow down to a point as the slope of the energy curve levels off as well

The green curve shows the average expected nuclear momentum between the two states taking into account how much each state is populated.

This graph shows the nuclear coherence between the bonding and antibonding states. The imaginary and real component of the coherence come from the complex nuclear wave function. The phase represents the interference between the two wavefunctions phases. We can see how the magnitude directly correlates to the maximum of the 2*<ψ1|p|ψ2><χ1|χ2> part of the total probability graph.

This plot shows the probability density of the electronic wavefunction corresponding to the superposition of the bonding and antibonding states as well as the expected nuclear dynamics. This density represents the likelihood that the electron will be found in a particular region of space. The density changes across time initially similar the pure electron dynamics, then as the two states nuclear overlap/coherence decreases it mostly becomes a combination of the <ψ1|p|ψ1><χ1|χ1> and <ψ2|p|ψ2><χ2|χ2> parts.

The purple dots represent the nuclei/protons in the H2+ bonding state. The yellow dots represent the nuclei/protons in the antibonding state

The two constants c1 and c2 represent the states of superposition where c1 represents the bonding state and c2 represents the antibonding state.

The orange graph shows the <ψ1|p|ψ1><χ1|χ1> part of the total probability chart. This part represents the probability density squared for the bonding electronic state wave function ψ1 multiplied by the probability density squared of the bonding nuclear state wave function χ1 radii. The green graph shows the <ψ2|p|ψ2><χ2|χ2> part of the total probability chart. This part represents the probability density squared for the antibonding electronic state wave function ψ2 multiplied by the probability density squared of the antibonding nuclear state wave function χ2 across all radii. The red graph shows the 2*<ψ1|p|ψ2><χ1|χ2> part of the total probability graph. This part represents the overlap of the bonding and antibonding state's electronic probability density wave functions ψ1 and ψ2 multiplied by the overlap of the bonding and antibonding state's nuclear probability density wave functions χ1 and χ2 across all radii

The blue graph shows the true electronic probability density function with respect to time which is the summation of the 3 parts. We can see that as the superposition states go to either 100% bonding or 100% antibonding that the probability density will become the respective part.
